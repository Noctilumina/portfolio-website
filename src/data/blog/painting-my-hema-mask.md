# Painting My HEMA Mask

So I decided to paint my fencing mask. It turned out to be a really fun little project. I'd been planning this for about two years, ever since I saw how cool everyone else's painted masks looked at my club. I was genuinely jealous of some of them.

## Why Paint It?

The main reason was practical: my face is quite expressive, especially during sword fighting. A painted mask hides that. Beyond that, I just thought it would look cool. Which it does. I think it looks great.

Someone at my club once told me that if you want pictures of yourself at tournaments, you need distinctive armor. That wasn't really the reason I did it, but it is a nice side effect. There are definitely more pictures of me now.

## The Design

The mask is meant to be a biblically accurate depiction of the goddess Iris. That's where the golden wings come from. The original plan also included rainbow colors coming out of the eye area, but the rainbow made the sun rays look broken and messy, so I scrapped that part.

I wanted it to feel a bit intimidating to opponents too. A bit aggressive. So I added a red eye to give it an Eye of Sauron vibe, which was one of the big inspirations.

The original stencil also had the name "Iris" in Old Greek, but the resolution of the mesh just didn't allow for legible text.

## The Full Process

Here's the actual step-by-step, because it's more involved than you might think.

**1. Tape everything.** And I mean everything. The entire mask gets taped. The only thing left exposed is the mesh you want to paint. If you don't need to paint the inside (I didn't), fill the inside completely with crumpled paper. Old newspapers, magazines, whatever you have. I bought a fresh pack of A4 and crumpled them up in there. This stops the spray paint from accidentally hitting any surface inside the mask.

**2. Prime it.** Basic spray paint primer over the whole mesh. Let it dry.

**3. Base colors.** I was lucky because my mask has a single iron bar running straight down the center, which gave me a perfect dividing line. I taped off one half, spray painted the other. Did the light side first, then the dark side. If your mask doesn't have a natural dividing line, you'll need to get creative with the tape.

![Base backgrounds on the mask](/images/blog/Mask_base_backgrounds.jpeg)

**4. Stencils.** Tape off the entire mask again. Prepare your stencil on paper, cut it out, hold it against the mask where it needs to go, and then use a Stanley knife to cut through the tape. This is extremely time-consuming but absolutely worth it. Nothing else gives you that level of detail on mesh.

![Stencils applied to the mask](/images/blog/Mask_stencils.jpeg)

**5. Drying.** Every single step needs to dry for a good while. About two hours per stage if I remember right. Don't rush this.

**6. Touch-ups with brushes.** After removing the tape, you'll find that paint sometimes bleeds underneath, or tape covered areas you didn't intend. Time to fix it with brushes. For me, outlining the wings in white was a blessing in disguise. It gave me really fine detail, and my white brush paint matched the spray paint perfectly.

![Outlining bands on the mask](/images/blog/Mask_band_outlining.jpeg)

**7. Detailing.** This is the longest part. The mask is a 3D surface, and if you're not using spray paint, regular paint has a hard time getting into all the little grooves where the mesh contacts itself. This took multiple days of work. Just patience and a steady hand.

**8. The band.** Last came the band around the mask with Vitruvian waves. I was a bit worried the mask might be interpreted as very Christian, given the biblically accurate angel on the front. The Hellenistic waves were my way of nudging people toward the Greek goddess Iris connection instead. The waves are simplified though: thick single waves rather than proper spirals. The mesh resolution doesn't allow for more.

**9. Seal it.** Spray the entire mask with a clear lacquer finish. Before that, sand off any thick bits of paint. Then apply the lacquer and let it dry. I dried mine under the hood of our furnace for better air circulation, which worked really well.

## Working With the Mesh

Nobody tells you this upfront: the resolution is terrible. But it turns out that if you keep painting consistent lines, treating the mesh as if it's paper underneath your brush, you can get surprisingly far. The mind fills in the gaps.

Geometric patterns work, but you need to scale them up. The grid isn't uniform because it covers a curved surface. It warps, especially at the edges. You can't just follow the grid in a repeating pattern because it'll go diagonal on you.

The human eye sees through black grids pretty easily. Your brain just filters it out. But white grids? Much harder to see through. So if you're painting bright colors, make sure the inside stays black. Mine already was, but if yours isn't, spray the inside black first.

## Paint Choice

Do NOT use metal-based paints. When a sword hits your mask, it scrapes up paint chips that can get in your face. With regular acrylic that's annoying but harmless. With metal paints, those chips can stick in your eyeball and rotate with it. Stick to acrylics.

I used spray paint for the base coat, wings, and central circle. All the finer details were done with acrylic brushes from Pipo's. Best combo I found.

## Credit Where It's Due

My partner Morgan helped me with a lot of this. I did not anticipate how time-consuming the stencil work would be on a curved mesh surface. It easily took two full weekends of doing nothing but this to get to the final result.

## Tips

- **Tape the whole mask.** Everything. Fill the inside with paper.
- **Prime first.** Don't skip this.
- **Thin coats.** Three thin coats beats one thick coat every time.
- **Use acrylic paint only.** No metal-based paints. Your eyes will thank you.
- **Spray paint for big areas, brushes for detail.**
- **Budget time.** This took two full weekends. Multiple days just for detailing.
- **Get help.** Stencil work on a curved surface is awkward.
- **Let every stage dry.** About two hours between stages.
- **Sand before sealing.** Remove thick bits, then lacquer.
- **Accept the resolution.** Bold, simple shapes read best on mesh.
- **Keep the inside black.** Bright mesh is hard to see through.

## Was It Worth It?

Absolutely. I'd been wanting to do this for two years and I'm really pleased with how it turned out. Now I have a mask that's genuinely mine. It looks cool, it hides my expressions during fights, and yes, it does get me noticed in tournament photos. Can't complain about that.

Would I do it again? Already planning the next design.

# The Cyberpunk Red GM Toolkit I Wish I Had When I Started

I've been DMing Cyberpunk Red for a while now. I can improvise encounters, make up NPCs on the spot, and keep a session moving. But that improvisation costs energy, and the more of it I burn on generating a random vendor's inventory or remembering the exact Night Market tables, the less I have for the stuff that actually makes a session great: reacting to player choices, building tension, doing voices.

I wanted to automate the mechanical busywork so I could focus on being a GM, not a rules reference. So I built a full toolkit inside my portfolio.

## The Encounter Generator

Four tabs: Encounters, NPCs, Loot, and Locations. Hit "Roll Again" and new content stacks on top, with older rolls fading out so you can keep a history.

**Random Encounters** give you an enemy type (Maelstrom, Arasaka operatives, MAX-TAC), count, threat level, scenario ("deal gone wrong in a parking garage"), and a tactical note ("enemies have a netrunner providing overwatch").

**NPC Generator** rolls a complete character: name with street handle, role, personality, motivation, cyberware loadout, and a full 10-stat block. "Vex 'Phantom' Nakamura, Fixer. Charismatic smooth-talker, building a criminal empire. Cyberware: Kerenzikov, Cybereye (targeting scope), Monowire."

**Loot Tables** are where it got serious. I wanted every weapon from the book with actual flavor text. Not just "Heavy Pistol" but "Malorian Arms 3516. Johnny Silverhand's custom piece. Only a handful ever made. Found: Legendary fixers only. If you have to ask, you can't afford it." The loot tab has a weapon type filter so you can say "show me all assault rifles" and get the full catalog with damage, quality, cost, and where to find them.

## The Night Market Generator (Following RAW)

This one follows the actual Cyberpunk Red rules exactly:

1. Roll 1d6 twice for two shop categories (Food & Drugs, Personal Electronics, Weapons & Armor, Cyberware, Clothing & Fashionware, Survival Gear)
2. Roll 1d10 per category for how many item types are available
3. Roll d100 per item on the category's table

Every entry shows the d100 roll, item name, cost, and price category. It matches what you'd get rolling on the actual tables, except instantly.

There's also a Midnight Market tab (Rank 9+ Fixer) that generates 1d10 + 5 rare items from all categories.

## The Encounter Balancer

You add your players with their key stats (REF, best combat skill, HP, armor SP, average weapon damage), hit "Analyze," and it generates five encounter difficulties based on the CPR rules:

| Difficulty | Formula |
|---|---|
| Easy | PCs - 1 Mooks |
| Medium | Equal Mooks to PCs |
| Hard | 1 Lieutenant per 2 PCs + Mooks |
| Very Hard | 1 Mini Boss per 3 PCs + Mooks |
| Deadly | Cyberpsycho (only if party is ready) |

It also analyzes the party for vulnerabilities: "2 PCs have low HP (<25), they can go down in 1-2 hits from heavy weapons" or "Large power gap between PCs, weaker characters may feel sidelined."

The NPC stat blocks match the book: Boosterganger (20 HP, SP 4), Security Officer (40 HP, SP 13), Cyberpsycho (55 HP, SP 11, skill 17).

## The Rules Wiki

I wrote a whole separate post about this one, but the short version: 100 wiki articles covering the entire rulebook, plus 400+ quick-reference procedures searchable by keyword with topic filter chips. Type "grapple" and get step-by-step. Type "death save" and get the exact mechanic. Filter by "Combat" or "Netrunning" to browse.

## The Data

The full equipment catalog has:
- **40+ ranged weapons** with manufacturer, damage, ROF, magazine, quality, cost, flavor text, and where to find them
- **10+ melee weapons** from combat knives to sledgehammers
- **14 exotic weapons** with full unique mechanics (Malorian 3516, Kendachi Mono-Three, Rhinemetall Railgun, etc.)
- **13 ammunition types** with effects
- **35+ cyberware pieces** organized by slot
- **27+ gear items** (electronics, medical, explosives, tools, armor, supplies)

All items have flavor text and a "Found:" field so you know where characters might actually acquire them in Night City.

## What I Learned

1. **Follow the actual rules.** My first Night Market generator just made stuff up. It worked fine for improv, but the book has specific tables that produce better variety than I'd come up with on the fly.
2. **Flavor text matters.** "Heavy Pistol, 3d6, 100eb" is forgettable. "Midnight Arms Borg. Massive hand cannon. Named for what it takes to handle the recoil" makes your players want it. Good flavor is free worldbuilding.
3. **Verification is critical.** I used LLMs to extract the rules from the PDF, and the first draft had death saves backwards and drug effects wrong. The mechanics were close enough that I'd been running them "approximately right" for sessions, but having the exact rules means fewer arguments and fairer outcomes.
4. **Automate the busywork, not the creativity.** I don't want a tool that runs my sessions for me. I want one that handles the tedious mechanical lookups so I can focus on reacting to my players, building atmosphere, and improvising the moments that make the game memorable.

If you're a CPR GM, the whole thing lives in my portfolio's Tools section. Roll some encounters, generate a Night Market, and let me know if you find any rules I got wrong.
